Couples retreat venues: the real kind, not the movie

July 20, 2026 · The Retreat Register

Trying to find a true couples retreat online can feel like a scavenger hunt. Half the results point to a certain Vince Vaughn movie. The other half? Resorts that simply tack "couples" onto their existing family package, hoping nobody notices.

It's a muddle, and it makes finding a truly intimate, adult-focused getaway harder than it should be. We think it's time to clear the air about what "couples-ready" really means.

A real couples retreat isn't just a standard room with two bathrobes. It's about privacy. Think detached units, cabins, or suites with private entrances, not just another door off a bustling hallway. It's an adults-focused atmosphere where quiet moments aren't interrupted by cannonball contests at the pool. Food should be on your schedule, not the resort's—no rigid dining times, just good eats when you want them. And activities? They should cater to two, without requiring you to join a group yoga session or a noisy mixer.

This niche is growing fast. BookRetreats' State of Retreats survey calls couples retreats the fastest-growing segment. Yet, many venues still miss the mark. The typical "romance package" often boils down to a bottle of cheap sparkling wine and a towel swan on the bed. That's not romance; that's an upsell. As of today, The Retreat Register holds 2,000+ verified retreat venues. While only 186 venues publish a starting price – pricing in this industry is quote-only by default – 91% of venues list a direct phone number, making it easier to inquire directly. We have deep coverage in places like California (163 venues), New York (98), and Florida (84), but finding those genuinely couples-ready spots still requires a discerning eye.

The problem for planners and venue owners alike is this: the market demands authentic couples experiences, but the term "couples retreat" is diluted. Planners struggle to find suitable venues, and venues miss opportunities by not truly understanding or marketing to this segment. The fix? Venues, truly invest in creating dedicated, private, and flexible offerings for two. Planners, look beyond the marketing fluff and ask the hard questions about unit privacy, dining flexibility, and genuinely intimate activities. And yes, ditch the cheap sparkling wine.
